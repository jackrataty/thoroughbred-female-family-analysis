Speed Isn't the Same as Value

Earlier, I focused on which female families produced an unusually large share of fast horses. That answers a narrow question: who can flash ability?

But there's a second question that matters just as much to owners and buyers: who converts speed into money?

Key idea: Many horses can hit a 90+ speed figure at least once. Fewer stay sound and consistent enough to turn that ability into earnings.

How I Measured "Converting Speed into Money"

  • Flash-capable group: horses that reached SF ≥ 90 at least once
  • Economic outcome: total lifetime earnings
  • Within the SF ≥ 90 group, horses are split into earnings quartiles: bottom 25% ("flash but broke"), middle 50%, top 25% ("flash that paid")

This is the uncomfortable truth: one out of every four horses that proved real ability (SF ≥ 90) failed economically. I'm not claiming I can see "injury" in the data — but earnings require starts, soundness, and survivability. Low earnings paired with real speed is the footprint of attrition.

The 9-h Twist: More Speed, Less Money

This is where the analysis becomes useful. In the earlier speed-only screen, 9-h looked attractive because it produced more fast horses than expected. But when we add earnings, 9-h tells a different story:

9-h produced speed — but it did not convert speed into money. In this crop, it behaves like a "flashy but fragile" family.

That doesn't contradict the earlier conclusion — it refines it. A family can be good at producing peak performance and still be weak at producing durable, economically productive runners.

Bottom Line

If your goal is to buy or breed for real-world value, the question isn't only "who can run fast once?" It's "who produces speed that holds together long enough to pay?" This is exactly the distinction that raw speed-only pages miss.
